1) Description of Measurement

The Kyphotic Angle measures the degree of segmental kyphosis across a fractured lumbar vertebra using the Cobb method. It reflects the angular deformity created by vertebral body collapse and is a key indicator of fracture instability, progression of deformity, and need for surgical correction.

2) Instructions to Measure

Imaging Requirements

  • High-resolution sagittal CT reconstruction including the fractured level and adjacent vertebrae.

    • Step-by-Step

  • Identify End Vertebrae
  • Select the intact vertebral body immediately above the fracture.
  • Select the intact vertebral body immediately below the fracture.
  • Draw Reference Lines
  • Draw a line along the superior endplate of the vertebra above the fracture.
  • Draw a line along the inferior endplate of the vertebra below the fracture.
  • Construct Perpendiculars
  • From each endplate line, draw a perpendicular.
  • Measure the Angle
  • Measure the angle at the intersection of the two perpendiculars.
  • Record the Cobb kyphotic angle (degrees).

    • 3) Normal vs. Pathologic Ranges

  • Normal/physiologic: 0-10°
  • Mild post-traumatic kyphosis: 10-20°
  • Moderate deformity: 20-30°
  • Severe kyphosis: > 30° (associated w/ mechanical pain, deformity progression, and neurologic risk)

    • Progressive increase on serial imaging suggests instability.

    5) Other info....

    Kyphotic angle should be measured in conjunction with:

  • Anterior/posterior height ratios.
  • Canal compromise percentage.
  • Sagittal index.

    • Progressive kyphosis >10° over time is a red flag for delayed instability.

    CT provides more accurate endplate definition than plain radiographs in acute trauma.
