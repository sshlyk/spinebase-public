1) Description of Measurement

The Meyerding Classification is a quantitative method used to grade lumbar spondylolisthesis based on the percentage of anterior translation of L5 over S1. The grading system is determined by dividing the superior endplate of S1 into four equal quarters and identifying how far the posterior-inferior corner of L5 has translated anteriorly relative to this subdivided sacral base.

When measured on flexion and extension radiographs, the Meyerding grade also provides insight into dynamic instability, as change in slip percentage between positions indicates loss of functional support from disc, facets, and ligamentous structures.

2) Instructions to Measure

  • Obtain Flexion and Extension Lateral Lumbar Radiographs
  • Use standing lateral films in maximal flexion and maximal extension.
  • Ensure clear visualization of L5–S1.

    •  

  • Identify the Reference Structures
  • Outline the superior endplate of S1.
  • Identify the posterior-inferior corner of L5—this is the key reference point for slip measurement.
  • Divide the Sacral Base
  • Draw a horizontal line along the superior endplate of S1.
  • Divide this line into four equal segments, which represent 0–25%, 25–50%, 50–75%, and 75–100% anterior translation.
  • Measure Slip Distance
  • From the posterior edge of the sacral base, measure horizontally to the posterior-inferior corner of L5.

  • Repeat for Flexion and Extension Views
  • Compare the two slip percentages.
  • An increase in slip percentage between flexion and extension indicates dynamic instability.

    • 3) Normal vs. Pathologic Ranges

  • Grade 0: 0% slip; normal alignment
  • Grade I: 1-25%; low-grade spondylolisthesis
  • Grade II: 26-50%; low-grade spondylolisthesis
  • Grade III: 51-75%; high-grade spondylolisthesis
  • Grade IV: 76-100%; high-grade spondylolisthesis
  • Grade V: > 100%; L5 completely anterior to sacrum

    • Dynamic instability thresholds:

  • Change in slip >3-4 mm between flexion and extension indicates instability
  • Change in slip > 10% (percentage based) = significant dynamic instability

    5) Other info....

    The Meyerding method is the standard system for grading spondylolisthesis and is widely used in deformity classification, preoperative planning, and postoperative evaluation.

    Flexion–extension imaging adds value by determining whether the slip is stable or unstable, guiding decisions on fusion versus decompression alone.

    Dynamic slip progression is often associated with:

  • Facet joint insufficiency
  • Disc height collapse
  • Ligamentous laxity

    • In high-grade slips, Meyerding classification should be supplemented with:

  • Slip Angle (Lumbosacral Kyphosis)
  • Pelvic incidence–slip angle relationship
  • Sagittal balance parameters

    • EOS imaging can improve measurement reproducibility in large deformities.

    You may document both values as:

  • “Flexion slip: XX% (Grade I)”
  • “Extension slip: YY% (Grade II)”
  • “Δ Slip: ZZ% → dynamic instability”
