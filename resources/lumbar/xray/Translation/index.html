1) Description of Measurement

Lumbar translation refers to the anterior-posterior displacement of one vertebral body relative to the adjacent vertebra during flexion and extension. It is used to assess lumbar segmental instability, commonly due to degenerative spondylolisthesis, facet arthropathy, or ligamentous incompetence.

Measurement is performed at the posterior vertebral body margin, comparing flexion and extension lateral radiographs.

2) Instructions to Measure

  • Obtain standing lateral lumbar spine X-rays in:
  • Maximal flexion
  • Maximal extension
  • Choose the level of interest (commonly L4–5 or L5–S1).
  • On each view:
  • Draw a vertical reference line along the posterior margin of the inferior vertebral body (e.g., L5).
  • Draw a second line along the posterior margin of the superior vertebral body (e.g., L4).
  • Measure the horizontal distance (mm) between the two lines
  • First in flexion, then in extension
  • Calculate dynamic translation = |Flexion distance − Extension distance|

    • 3) Normal vs. Pathologic Ranges

  • Normal alignment: < 3 mm; physiologic motion
  • Instability: ≥ 3 mm; suggestive of dynamic instability
  • Pathologic alignment: ≥ 4.5 mm or > 15% of vertebral body width; diagnostic lumbar instability

    • Key Point:

    Thresholds most often cited for surgical-grade instability: ≥ 4 mm or ≥ 8% vertebral body width at L4–L5
