1) Description of Measurement

Lumbar segmental angulation—also called segmental rotation—quantifies the angular change between two adjacent lumbar vertebrae when the spine moves from full flexion to full extension.

This dynamic measurement reflects the functional rotational mobility of each lumbar motion segment (e.g., L1–2 through L5–S1). Excessive segmental angulation is an established radiographic indicator of lumbar instability, typically caused by degenerative disc disease, facet incompetence, trauma, or post-fusion adjacent-segment motion.

Segmental angulation helps identify the specific level demonstrating abnormal rotation and complements translational instability measurements.

2) Instructions to Measure

  • Obtain Flexion and Extension Radiographs
  • Use standing lateral lumbar spine X-rays in maximal flexion and maximal extension.
  • Ensure clear visualization of the vertebral endplates from L1 through S1.
  • Identify the Motion Segment
  • For each level (e.g., L3–L4, L4–L5, L5–S1):
  • Identify the inferior endplate of the superior vertebra.
  • Identify the superior endplate of the inferior vertebra.
  • Draw Endplate Lines
  • On both flexion and extension views:
  • Draw a straight line along the inferior endplate of the upper vertebra.
  • Draw a straight line along the superior endplate of the vertebra below.
  • Measure the Intervertebral Angle
  • Using PACS angle tools, measure the angle between the two endplate lines in flexion.
  • Repeat on the extension film.
  • Angles typically open:
  • Anteriorly in flexion.
  • Posteriorly in extension.
  • Calculate Segmental Angulation:
  • Document Level of Maximal Instability
  • Report angulation at each level.
  • The largest value identifies the segment with the greatest rotational motion.

    • 3) Normal vs. Pathologic Ranges

  • Normal Physiologic Motion: ≤ 11°; indicates intact facets, disc, and posterior ligamentous complex
  • Pathologic/instability threshold: > 11°; diagnostic for lumbar rotational instability

    • Additional Guidelines:

  • Some guidelines suggest between 10-15° of angulation suggests instability
  • A focal increase in angulation compared to adjacent levels suggests localized structural failure.
  • Combined findings of >11° angulation AND ≥3–4 mm translation strongly support dynamic instability.

    5) Other info....

    Segmental angulation is most useful for evaluating:

  • Degenerative spondylolisthesis
  • Facet arthropathy
  • Disc collapse
  • Postoperative adjacent-segment motion
  • Traumatic ligamentous injury

    • Flexion–extension radiographs should only be performed after excluding acute fracture on CT.

    Excessive angulation often reflects:

  • Posterior ligamentous complex deficiency
  • Facet joint incompetence
  • Advanced disc degeneration

    • Use calibrated PACS tools to minimize parallax and magnification errors.
